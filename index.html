5 Common Reasons Why Your AC Is Leaking Water (And What You Can Do About It)

If you’ve found a puddle near your indoor unit, don’t panic—most leaks are easy to diagnose and stop.

Guide • Home Cooling Read time: 6–8 mins DIY + Pro Advice

When you walk into your living room and see a puddle of water near your AC unit, your first thought is usually panic. I’ve been there. You expect cool air and comfort, but instead you’re dealing with water damage and a potential repair bill. The truth? An air conditioner leaking water is one of the most common HVAC problems homeowners face. And the good news is—it’s often preventable.

In this guide, I’ll walk you through the five most common reasons why your AC leaks water, what you can do step by step, and when it’s time to call a pro.

1. A Clogged Condensate Drain Line (The #1 Culprit)

Your AC works by pulling humidity from the air. That moisture condenses on the evaporator coil, runs into the condensate pan, and exits through a drain line.

But here’s the problem: dust, algae, and sludge build up inside that narrow pipe. When the condensate drain line is clogged, water can’t flow outside. It backs up, spills over the pan, and suddenly you see water pooling around your unit.

How to fix it (step by step):
  1. Turn off your AC at the breaker.
  2. Locate the drain line outside your home—it’s usually a small PVC pipe dripping water.
  3. Attach a wet/dry vacuum to the end, seal it with a rag, and run it for a minute or two.
  4. Flush the line with distilled vinegar to kill algae.

Pro Tip: Do this every three months to prevent future leaks.

2. A Frozen Evaporator Coil

Ever noticed frost or ice on your indoor AC coil? That’s not normal—it’s a red flag. A frozen evaporator coil usually happens because of poor airflow (dirty air filters, blocked returns) or low refrigerant.

When ice forms, it eventually melts into the pan. But since it melts faster than the system can drain, it overflows. Boom, water leak.

Quick checklist:
  • Replace your air filter if it’s clogged.
  • Make sure vents aren’t blocked by furniture.
  • If ice is visible, shut off the AC and let it thaw completely.
  • If freezing keeps happening, you may have a refrigerant leak. That’s a job for an HVAC pro.

3. A Damaged or Rusted Drain Pan

If your system is more than 10 years old, check the drain pan. Over time, metal pans rust and plastic pans crack. Even a small crack means water escapes before it reaches the drain line.

What to do:
  • Use a flashlight to inspect the pan under the indoor unit.
  • If it’s rusty or cracked, replace it.
  • Make sure the pan is tilted slightly toward the drain outlet so water flows out naturally.

4. A Condensate Pump Failure

Not every AC drains by gravity. In basements and attics, units use a condensate pump to push water out. If the pump fails, the float switch may stick, or the discharge line gets blocked—and suddenly your pump is just sitting there, full of water.

Step-by-step fix:
  1. Unplug the pump and check for power.
  2. Remove the reservoir cover and clean out any gunk.
  3. Test the float by lifting it manually—if the pump doesn’t start, it’s faulty.
  4. Check the discharge line for clogs.

Safety tip: Many pumps have an overflow switch that shuts down the AC when water gets too high. If your AC randomly won’t turn on, this could be why.

5. Improper Installation or Leveling

Sometimes, the leak isn’t about wear and tear—it’s about setup. If you own a window AC or mini-split, installation angle matters.

  • Window units must be slightly tilted backward so water flows outdoors. Too level or tilted inside? Water drains into your room.
  • Mini-splits rely on a small drain hose. If it’s kinked, clogged, or disconnected, you’ll see drips along the wall.
What you can do:
  • For a window AC, check the slope. Manufacturers recommend a slight tilt.
  • Inspect mini-split drain hoses for blockages or improper routing.

FAQs: Why Is My AC Leaking Water?

Q1: Is water leaking from my AC dangerous?
Not directly—but it can cause mold, rot, and ceiling damage. Plus, leaks sometimes signal refrigerant issues.
Q2: Can I run my AC if it’s leaking?
Short-term, maybe. But I wouldn’t. Leaks often get worse, and running it can lead to costly damage.
Q3: How do I know if it’s a clog or a refrigerant problem?
If you see ice → likely refrigerant or airflow. No ice → likely a drain clog.
Q4: Should I call a pro?
If basic cleaning doesn’t solve it, yes—especially if you suspect refrigerant leaks or pump failures.
